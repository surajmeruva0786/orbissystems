Coursa is an AI-powered learning platform that converts any YouTube video, playlist, or channel into a fully structured online course. The platform sits between the world's largest free educational content library and the structured learning experience that people actually pay for on platforms like Udemy and Coursera.

The core problem Coursa solves is simple. YouTube has more educational content than any university on earth, and it is all free. But free content without structure produces passive watchers, not learners. People start a 40-video playlist, watch a few videos, get distracted, and never finish. There is no syllabus, no accountability, no quizzes, no progress tracking, and no credential at the end. Meanwhile, Udemy does $700 million in revenue every year selling structure around content that often exists free on YouTube. Coursa owns the space between these two worlds.

The user experience is straightforward. A user pastes any YouTube URL into Coursa — a single video, a playlist, or an entire channel. The platform pulls the transcript, passes it through an AI engine that reads and understands the content, and within 60 seconds generates a complete course. The output includes named modules, individual lessons, a summary for each lesson, quizzes at the end of each module, a recommended weekly study schedule, and a progress tracker. The user then follows a real curriculum, answers comprehension-based quizzes, tracks their streak, and works toward a verified certificate of completion.

The platform serves two distinct user types. The first is the self-directed learner — a professional upskilling, a student supplementing their education, or a career changer preparing for a new field. These users want to actually finish what they start and retain what they learn. The second and more valuable user is the YouTube educator or content creator. Any YouTuber with an existing library of educational videos can use Coursa's Creator Mode to package their entire channel into a structured paid course, set their own price, and sell it to their audience — without rebuilding anything on Teachable or Kajabi. Coursa handles the entire learning infrastructure.

The pricing model has three tiers. A free plan allows three courses per month with basic features and serves as the acquisition funnel. The Learner plan at twelve dollars per month gives unlimited courses, personalised syllabuses, and verified certificates. The Creator plan at forty-nine dollars per month adds white-labelling, the ability to sell courses to an audience, student analytics, and a revenue dashboard. The Creator plan is the primary revenue driver because a single YouTuber with a large audience brings hundreds of paying students onto the platform at once, and those students' progress data and the creator's course library create deep lock-in on both sides.

The moat builds over time across three layers. The first is a data moat — every course processed trains the AI to better understand pedagogical structure across different subjects, making the output progressively more accurate and structured than any generic LLM competitor. The second is a workflow integration moat — once a creator's audience is learning on Coursa and their course library lives on the platform, switching to a competitor means losing all student history, all progress data, and all the branding they have built. The third is a content graph moat — over time Coursa pre-processes the most popular educational YouTube channels and builds an instant course library, so new users searching for topics like Python or UI design get results immediately without waiting for processing. None of these moats exist on day one, but all three compound meaningfully between months six and twenty-four.

The technology stack is a YouTube transcript API for content ingestion, a large language model for curriculum generation and quiz creation, a web application frontend for the course experience, and a progress and certificate backend. The first version can be built in eight to twelve weeks focusing entirely on the core magic moment — paste a URL, get a structured course in sixty seconds.

The go-to-market strategy in year one runs on three channels. The first is organic community seeding on Reddit communities like r/learnprogramming, r/productivity, and r/GetMotivated, where the product can be demonstrated without a hard pitch. The second is direct outreach to mid-size YouTube educators with between fifty thousand and five hundred thousand subscribers, offering the Creator plan free for three months in exchange for them directing their audience to the platform. The third is SEO targeting high-intent searches like "learn Python from YouTube" or "best YouTube courses for UI design" where competition is weak and intent is strong.

The exit thesis targets a two to three year horizon. At one to three million in annual recurring revenue with strong net revenue retention, Coursa becomes an acquisition target for Udemy, Coursera, LinkedIn Learning, or a media company that wants to own the structured learning layer on top of YouTube. The primary acquisition assets are the creator relationships, the course content graph built from processing thousands of channels, and the learner completion data that no competitor can replicate from scratch. At eight to twelve times ARR the exit range sits between ten and thirty million dollars.
